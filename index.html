Latest Briefing / Apr 26, 2026

Eastern Europe's security problem is now a logistics problem.

The central question for Eastern European security is no longer whether NATO understands the threat. It does. The harder question is whether the alliance can convert that understanding into sustained movement: troops, air defense, surveillance, ammunition, infrastructure, and political consent over time.

Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine pushed European security away from symbolic reassurance and toward practical endurance. Deterrence now depends on whether the eastern flank can be reinforced quickly enough, protected across air and missile domains, and connected to the industrial capacity needed for a long confrontation.

1. Deterrence is measured in reinforcement, not language.

NATO's forward presence matters because it signals that an attack on the eastern flank would immediately involve multiple allies. But the real credibility test is sustainment. A battlegroup is a signal; reinforcement routes, air defense, interoperable command systems, and ammunition flows are the system that makes the signal believable.

2. Accountability is also a logistics problem.

ICC accountability and wider legal work around Ukraine depend on evidence chains, witness protection, state cooperation, data preservation, sanctions enforcement, and institutions that can hold cases together for years. The law does not move at battlefield speed, but it still needs infrastructure.

3. Ukraine connects both sides of the problem.

Ukraine is not only defending territory. It is also generating the evidence, memory, and strategic lessons that will shape the next European security order. If deterrence protects the future, accountability explains what the future is being protected from.

Policy read

  • NATO's eastern flank should be judged by mobility, air defense, and sustainment, not only by troop presence.
  • Accountability policy should be treated as strategic infrastructure, not a post-war afterthought.
  • The bridge between security and law is documentation: what is recorded now determines what can be defended, prosecuted, and remembered later.

Bottom line: Eastern Europe is becoming a test of whether democratic states can make long-term pressure look credible. The countries that can connect logistics, law, and public resolve will define the next phase of European security.
